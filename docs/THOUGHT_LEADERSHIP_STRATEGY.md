## Content Philosophy

### What McKinsey/BCG/Gartner Content Gets Right

1. **Lead with data, not opinion.** "67% of food manufacturers fail their first FSMA audit" > "FSMA audits are hard"
2. **Create frameworks.** People remember 2x2 matrices and maturity models. They screenshot and share them.
3. **Name the pattern.** Give a name to something everyone experiences but nobody has articulated. ("The Maintenance Paradox: why spending more on maintenance increases downtime")
4. **Benchmark against peers.** "If your OEE is below 65%, you're in the bottom quartile of discrete manufacturers." People want to know where they stand.
5. **Be specific about industries.** Generic "manufacturing" content gets ignored. "Meat processors spend 23% more on compliance than bakeries" gets shared.
6. **Never pitch.** The moment you mention your product, credibility drops to zero. The insight IS the selling.

### The Implicit Selling Model

```
Post about FSMA compliance gaps        → VP Food Safety thinks "this person gets it"
Post about predictive maintenance ROI  → VP Operations thinks "this is exactly my problem"
Send LinkedIn connection request       → They see your posts on their profile visit
They accept + you ask a question       → They respond because you've earned credibility
You mention what you're building       → They're already primed with trust
```

**The content doesn't sell. The content earns the right to have a conversation.**

## Content Formats

### Format 1: Data Insight Post (2x/week)
**Structure**: Hook stat → Context → "So what?" → Question to audience
**Length**: 800-1300 characters (LinkedIn sweet spot)
**Visual**: Single stat card or simple chart image

Example:
```
67% of FDA 483 observations cite "inadequate monitoring of critical control points."

Not equipment failure. Not contamination.
Documentation gaps.

The most common finding isn't that food manufacturers
aren't monitoring CCPs — it's that they can't PROVE
they monitored them consistently.

The paper trail problem:
• Manual logs get missed during shift changes
• Temperature excursions go unrecorded for hours
• Corrective actions lack timestamps
• Verification records aren't linked to specific CCPs

The companies that pass audits consistently
aren't the ones with better food safety practices.
They're the ones with better documentation systems.

What's your CCP documentation process look like?
Still paper-based or have you gone digital?
```

### Format 2: Framework/Model Post (1x/week)
**Structure**: Name the framework → Visual → Explain quadrants/levels → "Where do you fall?"
**Length**: 1000-1500 characters
**Visual**: 2x2 matrix or maturity model diagram (clean, minimal, professional)

Example framework: "The Maintenance Maturity Matrix"
```
         Low Data Infrastructure  │  High Data Infrastructure
        ─────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────
High    │ "Experienced but       │ "Predictive Leaders"
Org     │  Flying Blind"         │  (Top 15%)
Maturity│ Run-to-failure with    │  Sensor data + ML models
        │ expert intuition       │  + closed-loop work orders
        ─────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────
Low     │ "Reactive & Struggling"│ "Data Rich, Insight Poor"
Org     │ (Bottom 40%)           │  (Common trap)
Maturity│ No process, no data    │  Sensors installed but
        │                        │  nobody looks at dashboards
```

### Format 3: Contrarian Take (1x/week)
**Structure**: State the conventional wisdom → Explain why it's wrong → Provide the better frame
**Length**: 600-1000 characters
**Visual**: None needed (text-only posts often perform best for opinions)

Example:
```
Unpopular opinion: Most predictive maintenance programs
fail because they're solving the wrong problem.

The pitch is always "predict failures before they happen."

But the real bottleneck at most manufacturers isn't
prediction accuracy — it's what happens AFTER the prediction.

If your maintenance team gets a "bearing failure in 12 days"
alert but:
• There's no spare in inventory
• The tech with that skill is on vacation
• The work order system isn't connected
• Nobody trusts the alert because the last 5 were false

...then a perfect prediction is worthless.

The companies that get ROI from PdM don't start with models.
They start with their work order process.

Agree or disagree?
```

### Format 4: Benchmark/Survey Post (monthly)
**Structure**: "We analyzed X" → Key findings → Implications → Download link
**Length**: 1200-1500 characters
**Visual**: 2-3 chart images in carousel format

Example:
```
We analyzed 200+ FDA warning letters from 2024-2025
to find the most common citations for food manufacturers.

Top 5 findings:

1. Inadequate HACCP plan (34%)
2. CCP monitoring gaps (28%)
3. Corrective action failures (19%)
4. Sanitation deficiencies (12%)
5. Supplier verification gaps (7%)

The surprising finding: 62% of citations
were DOCUMENTATION problems, not actual
food safety failures.

The food was fine. The paperwork wasn't.

[Chart showing trend over 3 years]

Implications for mid-market food manufacturers:
The biggest risk isn't contamination.
It's not being able to prove you prevented it.
```

## Visual Style Guide

### Design Principles (McKinsey/BCG standard)
- **Clean, minimal, professional** — no clip art, no stock photos, no gradients
- **Maximum 2 colors** — dark navy (#1e293b) + one accent (Digitillis blue #3b82f6)
- **White/light gray backgrounds** — never dark backgrounds for data visuals
- **San-serif fonts** — Inter or similar
- **Data always sourced** — "Source: FDA CFSAN, 2024-2025 Warning Letters" in small text
- **No Digitillis branding on visuals** — your name only, not the company logo. This is thought leadership, not marketing.

### Visual Types
1. **Stat Card**: Single large number + context line + source. Clean white card.
2. **2x2 Matrix**: Named quadrants, thin grid lines, dot showing "most companies are here"
3. **Maturity Model**: 4-5 horizontal levels, labeled, with indicator of typical position
4. **Simple Bar/Line Chart**: Max 5 bars. Clear labels. No 3D effects. Source noted.
5. **Carousel Slides**: 3-5 slides, consistent template, title + insight + visual per slide

### Image Generation Approach
For each post, generate a companion image using:
- Structured data → simple chart (can use matplotlib/seaborn, exported as clean PNG)
- Frameworks → diagram (can use Figma template, Canva, or code-generated SVG)
- Stat cards → HTML/CSS → screenshot (fastest, most consistent)

## Quality Standards (Non-Negotiable)

### Every Post Must:
- [ ] Lead with a specific number, data point, or bold claim
- [ ] Be factually accurate and sourceable (no made-up statistics)
- [ ] Provide genuine value a reader can use TODAY
- [ ] End with a question that invites comments
- [ ] Be readable on mobile (short paragraphs, line breaks)
- [ ] NOT mention Digitillis, AI platforms, or any product
- [ ] NOT use hashtags (they reduce LinkedIn organic reach in 2026)
- [ ] NOT be longer than 1500 characters (LinkedIn truncates at ~1300)
- [ ] NOT use em dashes, "moreover", "furthermore", or other AI tells
- [ ] NOT include stock imagery or clip art

### Every Visual Must:
- [ ] Be clean and professional (McKinsey presentation quality)
- [ ] Use max 2 colors (navy + accent blue)
- [ ] Include data source attribution
- [ ] NOT include Digitillis logo or branding
- [ ] Be optimized for LinkedIn's 1200x627 or 1080x1080 dimensions

## ROI of Thought Leadership

### Direct Impact
- LinkedIn connection acceptance rate: 15-20% (cold) → 30-40% (with active content)
- DM response rate: 5-10% (cold) → 15-25% (they've seen your posts)
- Email open rate: Content-active senders get 10-15% higher opens (name recognition)

### Compound Effect (Over 90 Days)
- 48 posts × average 500 impressions = 24,000 impression-events
- 5% of impressions from ICP targets = 1,200 touches on decision-makers
- These touches cost $0 and build credibility that money can't buy
- By month 3: inbound inquiries start appearing ("Saw your posts about FSMA...")

### The McKinsey Playbook
McKinsey publishes 500+ articles/year. Not because each one generates leads.
Because the collective body of work creates an unassailable position:
"These are the smartest people in the room about this topic."

That's what 4 posts/week for 90 days builds for you in manufacturing operations + food safety.
